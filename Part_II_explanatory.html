Part II - The Anatomy of U.S. Flight Delays

by Tyler Wilcoxson

Investigation Overview

This investigation explores the patterns and causes of flight delays in U.S. domestic air travel. Using approximately 2 million flight records from the Bureau of Transportation Statistics' Reporting Carrier On-Time Performance database, I examined when, why, and how delays occur -- and what travelers and airlines can learn from these patterns.

Dataset Overview and Executive Summary

The dataset contains detailed information for each flight including the airline, origin and destination airports, scheduled and actual departure/arrival times, delay duration in minutes, and a breakdown of delay causes (carrier, weather, National Airspace System, security, and late aircraft).

Key Findings:

  1. Delays are predictable by time of day. Flights departing in the early morning (05:00-08:00) experience significantly fewer delays than those departing in the late afternoon and evening (16:00-21:00). Delays cascade throughout the day as aircraft fall behind schedule.

  2. Airlines partially recover lost time in the air. Departure delays are very strongly correlated with arrival delays, but airlines recover an average of several minutes during flight. This recovery is insufficient for large delays.

  3. Late aircraft delays are the dominant systemic cause. Among flights delayed 15 or more minutes, cascading delays from earlier flights ("late aircraft") contribute the most total delay minutes, followed by carrier-related and NAS delays.

  4. Summer and winter months have the highest delay rates. Thunderstorm season (June-August) and winter weather (December-February) drive seasonal peaks in both weather-specific and overall delay rates.

Visualization 1: When Do Delays Strike?

Delays are not randomly distributed throughout the day. They follow a predictable pattern driven by the cascading nature of airline operations. Aircraft that depart late in the morning continue to fall further behind schedule as the day progresses. This heatmap reveals the average arrival delay for every combination of departure hour and day of the week.

ax.set_ylabel('Day of Week', fontsize=13)

# Add insight annotation
ax.text(0.5, -0.18, 'Insight: Book early morning flights (5-8 AM) for the most reliable schedules.',
        transform=ax.transAxes, fontsize=11, fontstyle='italic',
        ha='center', color='#555555')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Early morning flights (5-8 AM) consistently show the lowest average delays across all days of the week. As the day progresses, delays build steadily, reaching their peak between 4 PM and 9 PM. This "cascade effect" happens because aircraft fly multiple segments per day -- a delay on the first flight ripples through all subsequent flights using that aircraft. Friday and Sunday evenings are particularly prone to delays, coinciding with business and leisure travel peaks.

Visualization 2: The Cascade Effect -- Departure Delay vs. Arrival Delay

When a flight departs late, does it arrive equally late? Or can airlines make up time in the air? This density plot examines the relationship between departure and arrival delays across all flights in the dataset.

both_valid = df[df['DepDelay'].notna() & df['ArrDelay'].notna()]

The relationship between departure and arrival delays is remarkably strong (r > 0.9). Most of the density falls just below the diagonal reference line, meaning airlines do recover some lost time during flight -- on average about 5 minutes. However, this recovery is modest: a flight that departs 60 minutes late will still arrive roughly 55 minutes late. The hexbin density clearly shows the concentration of on-time flights near (0, 0), with a thin but long tail of severely delayed flights extending to the upper right.

Visualization 3: What Causes Delays?

Not all delays are created equal. The BTS categorizes delays into five causes: carrier-related, weather, National Airspace System (NAS), security, and late aircraft. Understanding which causes contribute the most total delay minutes reveals where the biggest improvement opportunities lie.

In[5]:
# Polished Visualization 3: Delay cause breakdown by top airlines
delay_cols = ['CarrierDelay', 'WeatherDelay', 'NASDelay',
              'SecurityDelay', 'LateAircraftDelay']
cause_labels = ['Carrier', 'Weather', 'NAS', 'Security', 'Late Aircraft']

# Get top 8 airlines by flight volume
top8 = df['Reporting_Airline'].value_counts().head(8).index.tolist()
delayed = df[df[delay_cols].notna().all(axis=1) &
             df['Reporting_Airline'].isin(top8)].copy()

# Compute mean delay by cause for each airline
cause_by_airline = []
for col, label in zip(delay_cols, cause_labels):
    group = delayed.groupby('Reporting_Airline')[col].mean().reset_index()
    group.columns = ['Airline', 'AvgDelay']
    group['Cause'] = label
    cause_by_airline.append(group)
cause_df = pd.concat(cause_by_airline, ignore_index=True)

# Order airlines by total mean delay across all causes
airline_order = (cause_df.groupby('Airline')['AvgDelay'].sum()
                 .sort_values(ascending=False).index.tolist())

# Define color palette
cause_palette = {'Late Aircraft': '#e74c3c', 'Carrier': '#e67e22',
                 'NAS': '#3498db', 'Weather': '#2ecc71',
                 'Security': '#95a5a6'}

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 7))
sns.barplot(data=cause_df, x='Airline', y='AvgDelay', hue='Cause',
            order=airline_order,
            hue_order=['Late Aircraft', 'Carrier', 'NAS', 'Weather', 'Security'],
            palette=cause_palette, edgecolor='white', ax=ax)

ax.set_title('Late Aircraft and Carrier Issues Drive Most Delays',
             fontsize=16, fontweight='bold', pad=15)
ax.set_xlabel('Airline', fontsize=13)
ax.set_ylabel('Average Delay Minutes (Delayed Flights Only)', fontsize=13)
ax.legend(title='Delay Cause', bbox_to_anchor=(1.02, 1), loc='upper left',
          frameon=True, edgecolor='#cccccc')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Across all major airlines, late aircraft delays (shown in red) are consistently the largest contributor to delay minutes among delayed flights. This reflects a systemic challenge in hub-and-spoke airline networks: when one flight arrives late, the aircraft assigned to the next departure is unavailable, creating a cascade of delays through the day. Carrier-related delays (maintenance, crew, fueling) are the second-largest cause. Weather and NAS delays vary more by airline, depending on which hubs an airline operates from. Security delays are negligible for all carriers.

# Polished Visualization 4: Delay rate by month for top 5 airlines
top5 = df['Reporting_Airline'].value_counts().head(5).index.tolist()
valid = df[df['ArrDel15'].notna() & df['Reporting_Airline'].isin(top5)]

monthly_rate = (valid.groupby(['MonthName', 'Reporting_Airline'])['ArrDel15']
                .mean().reset_index())
monthly_rate['DelayPct'] = monthly_rate['ArrDel15'] * 100

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 7))
palette = sns.color_palette('Set2', n_colors=5)

for i, airline in enumerate(top5):
    subset = monthly_rate[monthly_rate['Reporting_Airline'] == airline]
    ax.plot(subset['MonthName'], subset['DelayPct'],
            marker='o', linewidth=2.5, markersize=7,
            label=airline, color=palette[i])

# Shade summer and holiday periods
ax.axvspan(4.5, 7.5, alpha=0.08, color='orange', label='Summer Peak')
ax.axvspan(10.5, 11.5, alpha=0.08, color='blue', label='Holiday Peak')

ax.set_title('Summer and Holiday Months See Highest Delay Rates Across All Carriers',
             fontsize=16, fontweight='bold', pad=15)
ax.set_xlabel('Month', fontsize=13)
ax.set_ylabel('Percentage of Flights Delayed (>15 min)', fontsize=13)
ax.legend(title='Airline', loc='upper left', frameon=True, edgecolor='#cccccc')
ax.set_ylim(bottom=0)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

All five major airlines follow the same seasonal pattern: delay rates rise during the summer months (June-August) when thunderstorms are most frequent across the central and eastern United States, and again in December during holiday travel and winter weather. The fall months (September-November) are consistently the best time to fly with the lowest delay rates. While the seasonal shape is similar for all carriers, the absolute level varies -- some airlines consistently run 5-10 percentage points higher or lower than their competitors, reflecting differences in operational efficiency, fleet age, and hub geography.

Conclusion: Flight delays in the U.S. are highly systematic. They follow predictable patterns by time of day, season, and cause. The single most actionable insight for travelers is to book early morning departures -- these flights haven't yet been affected by the cascading delays that build throughout the day. For airlines, the data suggests that improving aircraft turnaround times and schedule buffer management (to reduce late aircraft delays) would have the largest impact on overall on-time performance.
